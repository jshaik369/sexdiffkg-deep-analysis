## Abstract

The structure of drug safety knowledge graphs encodes information about sex-differential
adverse event patterns. Analyzing the SexDiffKG (109,867 nodes, 1,822,851 edges), we
demonstrate that network topology predicts sex bias: drug node degree positively correlates
with female bias (ρ = 0.182, p = 10⁻¹⁷), adverse event node degree correlates similarly
(ρ = 0.184, p = 6 × 10⁻⁴⁰), and drug-AE co-occurrence predicts sex profile similarity
(ρ = -0.107, p = 10⁻⁵¹). Sex bias is modular: female-biased drugs preferentially associate
with female-biased AEs (24.4% vs 4.1% expected by chance). In contrast, drug-target count
shows no sex correlation (ρ = -0.006, p = 0.86), suggesting that sex-differential safety
emerges from downstream pharmacological effects rather than target promiscuity. The top
drug hub (adalimumab, 8,203 edges, 64.0%F) exemplifies the degree-sex relationship.
Drug phenotype clustering reveals a continuous sex spectrum (10 clusters spanning 48.6-69.6%F)
rather than discrete male/female categories. These findings establish that sex-differential
drug safety is encoded in network structure and can be partially predicted from topology alone.
