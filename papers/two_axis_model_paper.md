## Abstract

We present a two-axis model showing that female predominance in pharmacovigilance signals 
is jointly determined by signal strength (|log ratio|) and report volume. Analysis of 
96,281 sex-differential signals reveals a dose-response gradient on both axes: the weakest 
signals are 63.5% female while the strongest are 87.4% female (strength axis); signals 
with fewest reports are 50.4% female while those with most reports are 82.0% female 
(volume axis). When both axes are maximized simultaneously—strong signals with many 
reports—female fraction reaches 96%. This two-axis framework explains why the anti-
regression phenomenon (female bias intensifying with volume) and the signal-strength 
gradient operate independently, and predicts that only weak signals with few reports 
approach sex balance. These findings fundamentally reshape our understanding of sex bias 
in drug safety surveillance and provide a quantitative framework for adjusting signal 
detection algorithms.

**Keywords:** pharmacovigilance, sex differences, dose-response, signal detection, FAERS, 
two-axis model

## 1. Introduction

Previous work has documented two puzzling phenomena in sex-differential pharmacovigilance:
(1) the anti-regression effect, where female bias intensifies rather than regresses toward 
the mean with increasing report volume; and (2) the observation that stronger signals 
(higher |log ratio|) tend to be more female-biased. These phenomena have been treated 
as independent observations. Here we show they are two manifestations of a single 
underlying two-axis model.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Data
96,281 sex-differential drug-adverse event signals from 14,536,008 FAERS reports 
(2004Q1-2025Q3). Each signal has: (1) a log ratio measuring direction and magnitude 
of sex differential, and (2) a total report count across both sexes.

### 2.2 Axis Analysis
Signals were binned into deciles along each axis independently, and female fraction 
computed per decile. A 2D landscape was generated by cross-tabulating signal strength 
bins (6 levels) with report volume bins (6 levels).

### 2.3 Direction Asymmetry
Female-higher and male-higher signals were compared for mean signal strength using 
Mann-Whitney U test.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 The Strength Axis
Signal strength deciles show monotonic increase in female fraction:
- D0 (|LR|=0.50-0.55): 63.5%F
- D5 (|LR|=0.81-0.92): 70.5%F
- D9 (|LR|=1.65-8.87): 87.4%F

### 3.2 The Volume Axis
Report volume deciles also show monotonic increase:
- D0 (20-29 reports): 50.4%F
- D5 (73-95 reports): 55.9%F
- D9 (464-55,041 reports): 82.0%F

### 3.3 The 2D Landscape
When both axes are cross-tabulated:
- Low strength + low volume: ~50-52%F (near balanced)
- Low strength + high volume: 68-75%F
- High strength + low volume: 35-50%F (can be male-biased!)
- **High strength + high volume: 93-96%F** (extreme female dominance)

### 3.4 Direction Asymmetry
Female-higher signals (53.8%) have significantly stronger signal magnitude than 
male-higher signals (46.2%): mean |LR| 1.007 vs 0.963 (p=2.80×10⁻⁴¹). 
Among extreme signals (|LR|≥3), 58.5% are female-directed.

### 3.5 Bootstrapped Confidence
Bootstrap (1,000 iterations): overall %F = 58.07% (95% CI: 57.93-58.21%). 
Anti-regression rho = 1.000 (CI: 0.988-1.000).

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 A Unified Framework
The two-axis model unifies previously disparate observations:
- Anti-regression is the volume axis effect
- Signal strength correlation is the strength axis effect
- Together they create a 2D landscape predicting female fraction from any 
  signal's coordinates

### 4.2 The Corner Cases
The 2D landscape reveals a critical asymmetry: high-strength + low-volume 
signals can be male-biased (35-50%F), while no combination of parameters 
produces male-biased HIGH-volume signals. This suggests that volume-driven 
female bias operates through reporting dynamics, while strength-driven patterns 
may reflect genuine biological sex differences.

### 4.3 Implications for Signal Detection
Current pharmacovigilance algorithms treat all signals equally regardless of 
sex distribution. Our two-axis model provides:
1. **Adjusted thresholds**: Signals in the high-strength, high-volume corner 
   should be interpreted with sex-specific adjustments
2. **Male-signal recovery**: True male-biased signals exist primarily in the 
   high-strength, low-volume corner—these may be systematically under-detected
3. **Calibration standard**: The 50%F baseline at low volume provides a natural 
   calibration point

## 5. Conclusion

The two-axis model demonstrates that sex-differential pharmacovigilance is 
governed by the joint effects of signal strength and report volume, with female 
predominance reaching 96% when both are maximized. This framework provides the 
first quantitative prediction tool for sex bias in drug safety signals.
